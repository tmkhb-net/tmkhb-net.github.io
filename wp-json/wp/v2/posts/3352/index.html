\nWhat is Composting?<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nComposting is the process of recycling organic materials such as food scraps, garden waste, and paper products. Instead of being sent to landfills, these materials are broken down by microorganisms to create a nutrient-rich soil amendment known as compost. Composting is a great way to reduce the amount of waste produced, as it can divert up to 50% of organic waste from landfills. Additionally, composting can provide numerous benefits to the environment. It can reduce greenhouse gas emissions, improve water and air quality, and replenish soil nutrients.
\n\u00a0
\nDespite these benefits, composting is often overlooked as a waste management solution. This is due to a variety of factors, including the lack of knowledge about composting and the perceived difficulty of setting up a composting system. However, composting does not have to be difficult. There are numerous types of composting systems, ranging from simple backyard composting to complex industrial systems. Additionally, there are a variety of resources available to help individuals get started with composting, such as composting classes and composting kits.
\n\u00a0

By making composting more accessible and encouraging more people to get involved, we can reduce the amount of waste produced and create a more sustainable future.

\nWhat is compost? \u2013 Compost Basics<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nCompost is just organic matter that has decomposed; nevertheless, the term \u201corganic matter\u201d has a rather broad definition. Organic debris can include twigs and banana peels alike. These things naturally decompose when combined in a compost pile to produce a fertilizer that is rich in nutrients and supports the growth of gardens.
\n\u00a0
\nWhat to Compost?<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nTypes of Matter<\/strong>
\nBrown matter is anything dry or woody, such as twigs, branches, paper, and cardboard. There are certainly exceptions to the general rule that \u201cbrown\u201d goods will often be naturally brown. Brown matter contains carbon, which gives the compost\u2019s helpful bacteria energy.
\n\u00a0
\nGreen matter is a term used to describe organic waste materials that may have recently been growing and still contain some moisture. Green matter can contain items like leftover fruit and vegetables, grass clippings, and coffee grounds, among other things.
\n\u00a0
\nNote \u2013 Most of the time, your green waste will provide your compost mixture with the right amount of moisture for effective decomposition, but if it is too dry, you might need to add extra water.
\n\u00a0
\nWhat Not to Compost?<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nThere are (nearly) unlimited possibilities for objects you may compost, but there are a few things you should avoid doing so if you want to end up with a compost that makes an effective and secure fertilizer (beyond the obvious, like plastic products).
\n\u00a0
\nFish and Meat<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nAnimal byproducts, including meat, fish, eggs, dairy, and poultry products, are one of the largest groups. Even while you might assume it\u2019s okay to compost certain kinds of animal waste, doing so can encourage the growth of bacteria, odor issues, and pests like rodents.
\n\u00a0
\nOils, Fats, and Dairy<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nBecause they also draw pests, dairy products including milk, sour cream, yogurt, cheese, and butter shouldn\u2019t be composted. The same holds for fats and oils.
\n\u00a0
\nCharcoal Ash and Coal<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nYou should carefully evaluate what you compost and the possible harm it might cause to your plants if your ultimate objective is to create a usable fertilizer for your plants. By the time you use your fertilizer, your vegetable garden may already be dead since items like coal or charcoal ash may contain compounds toxic to plants.
\n\u00a0
\nAmount of Black Walnut Tree Debris<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nThe toxin juglone, which is known to damage numerous plants, including peppers, potatoes, and tomatoes, is present in black walnut tree leaves and twigs.
\n\u00a0
\nPesticide-Treated Plants or Wood<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nAdditionally, you should avoid trimmings from plants that have received pesticide treatment in the past.
\n\u00a0
\nPlants that are ill or are pest-infested<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nPests and pathogens can survive since a compost pile has to reach 141 to 145 degrees F for at least several days to kill fungus and bacteria, which is not practical for a residential compost bin.
\n\u00a0
\nGrass That Has Sprouted<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nUnless a high temperature is reached, they can also live, and you probably don\u2019t want to plant weeds in your subsequent crop.
\n\u00a0
\nCat or Dog Excreta<\/strong>
\n\u00a0
\nPet waste contains germs and parasites that can lead to human illnesses, making compost dangerous. For instance, the bacterium that causes toxoplasmosis is found in cat faces and litter. Pregnant women should pay special attention to this condition because it can adversely harm an unborn child.
