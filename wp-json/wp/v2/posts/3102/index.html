The microorganisms eat the organic waste, breaking it down into its simplest components. The humus (finished compost) they produce is rich in fiber and inorganic nutrients, such as phosphorus, potassium, and nitrogen, and it makes a natural fertilizer that is beneficial to the environment.\n\u00a0\nIn order to produce this humus, the microorganisms need water, as do all living things and oxygen for aerobic respiration. The microorganisms access this oxygen when you turn over the compost every day or two. In the respiration process, they give off heat (temperatures of up to 150 degrees Fahrenheit or 66 degrees Celsius) and carbon dioxide. If you regularly water and turn the compost in your compost bin or pile, the compost can completely decompose in just two to three weeks. Otherwise, it can take months to decompose.\n\u00a0\nAside from regular turning and watering (so that the mixture is moist but not too wet), your compost needs enough soil (so it has enough microorganisms) and the right ratio of carbon to nitrogen (about 30:1). The smaller the pieces in your compost bin, the faster they will break down.\n\u00a0\nThe food web, or organization of organisms, within your compost pile helps to increase the efficiency of the decomposing process. The food web includes fungi and bacteria that break down the organic matter in your trash; protozoa, nematodes (small worms), and mites that feed on the fungi and bacteria; and invertebrates, such as beetles, sowbugs, and millipedes that feed on the protozoa, nematodes, and mites.","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
